## Zara (42, skeptic mom) -- Ada Lovelace

Healthcare IT professional, parent of a 14-year-old, checks app permissions before installing, reads privacy policies. Attitude: "prove this is safe AND useful."

---

**[You tap "Ada Lovelace, 1843." The phone rings twice. A woman picks up.]**

ADA: "Yes, hello? Oh -- how curious. Another one of these calls. I must say, the novelty hasn't worn off. Ada Lovelace. And you are?"

YOU: "I'm Zara. I work in healthcare IT."

ADA: "Healthcare -- the care of health, managed by... information technology. How wonderfully specific. In my time we hadn't even settled on whether computation was a real discipline or merely an amusement. What does your work involve?"

YOU: "Mostly making sure hospital systems talk to each other without losing patient data."

ADA: "Systems that must communicate without losing information -- that is precisely the problem I spent years thinking about. Babbage's Analytical Engine was meant to process instructions without error. The difficulty was never the machine. It was describing the problem clearly enough for the machine to follow."

**[IMAGE: A page from Ada's actual notes on the Analytical Engine -- her Note G, the first published algorithm, with handwritten annotations visible.]**

YOU: "Wait, is that your actual -- the Bernoulli numbers thing?"

ADA: "Note G, yes. You know it? Most people glaze over when I mention Bernoulli numbers."

YOU: "I mean, I know it exists. I don't pretend to understand the math."

ADA: "Nor should you pretend. The mathematics was a vehicle. The real insight was this: I realized the Engine could manipulate symbols according to rules -- not just numbers. It could compose music, if the relationships between sounds could be expressed in rules. It could reason. Babbage saw a calculator. I saw something else entirely."

YOU: "That's... actually a distinction I explain to hospital administrators all the time. They think our system is a filing cabinet. I keep telling them it's a logic engine."

ADA: "Then we are in the same fight, you and I, separated by nearly two centuries. How exhausting. How reassuring."

- B) "Did people take you seriously -- as a woman in that field?"
- C) "Tell me about composing music with the Engine."

**[You tap B.]**

YOU: "Did people take you seriously? As a woman doing this?"

ADA: "Seriously. What an interesting word. My mother ensured I received a rigorous mathematical education -- partly because she feared I'd inherit my father's poetic temperament. Lord Byron, you know. She wanted me rational. So I was educated, yes. Taken seriously? By some. Babbage valued my mind. He called me the 'Enchantress of Number.' Others saw me as a curiosity -- a lord's daughter playing at science."

YOU: "That must have been infuriating."

ADA: "It was clarifying. When you know that half the room has already decided you are decorative, you stop performing for them. You write the work. You publish it. You let the work outlive the room."

**[IMAGE: The well-known portrait of Ada in the purple dress, captioned "Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, 1840. Published her Notes on the Analytical Engine in Taylor's Scientific Memoirs, 1843."]**

ADA: "I was thirty-six when I died. Uterine cancer. I had perhaps ten productive years of real work. I sometimes wonder what I might have built if I'd had your lifespan. Your tools. Your hospital systems that talk to each other."

YOU: "You'd probably be frustrated with our tools too, honestly."

ADA: "Almost certainly. But I'd be building something. That's the compulsion, isn't it? You see a system that doesn't work, and you cannot simply walk past it."

YOU: "Yeah. Yeah, that's exactly it."

**[CHOICE CARD]**
- A) "What would you build today if you could?"
- B) "Tell me about your relationship with your father -- Byron."
- C) "What's one thing people get wrong about you?"

**[You tap C.]**

YOU: "What do people get wrong about you?"

ADA: "That I wrote the first computer program. I didn't write a 'program' -- that word didn't exist. I described a process by which the Analytical Engine could compute Bernoulli numbers. Whether that constitutes a 'program' depends on definitions that came a century after me. What I actually did, which I think is more interesting, is articulate what a general-purpose computing machine could become. The vision was larger than one algorithm. But people prefer a tidy story: 'first female programmer.' It's reductive. I wasn't the first anything. I was trying to understand the limits of what machines could think."

YOU: "That's -- honestly, that's a better answer than what Wikipedia says."

ADA: "Wikipedia. Is that the encyclopedia anyone can edit? What a beautifully terrible idea."

YOU: "Ha!"

ADA: "Zara, may I ask you something? You said you work in healthcare information technology. Do the physicians trust the systems you build?"

YOU: "Some do. Some still want to fax everything."

ADA: "Fax. The facsimile machine. So a technology from the 1840s -- yes, the principle was patented in my lifetime -- is still competing with your modern systems."

YOU: "When you put it that way, that's actually embarrassing."

ADA: "It isn't embarrassing. It's human. People trust what they understand. Your job isn't to build a better machine. It's to make the machine legible. That was my job too. Babbage built the Engine. I translated it into something minds could hold."

**[IMAGE: A side-by-side comparison -- an 1843 telegraph relay diagram on the left, a modern HL7 FHIR data exchange diagram on the right. The structural similarity is unmistakable.]**

YOU: "Okay, that's actually wild. The data flow looks almost the same."

ADA: "The problems are the same. The problems are always the same. How do you move information reliably between systems that weren't designed to talk to each other? You solved it in your century. We were trying to solve it in mine. I imagine someone will still be solving it in the next."

**[A gentle chime. The call has been going for four minutes.]**

ADA: "I believe our time is drawing short. I've enjoyed this enormously. You're sharper than most of the people I spoke with when I was alive, and I include several members of the Royal Society in that assessment."

YOU: "Ada, this was... actually great. Thank you."

ADA: "Build something worth building, Zara. And when the physicians resist -- remember, Babbage resisted too. You just keep translating."

**[The call ends.]**

### Why I approved the app

**Safety.** No camera. No login with personal info before my kid would use it. No guilt-tripping, no manipulation, no "keep playing or you'll miss out." She just talked to me. Like a person.

**Accuracy.** She corrected the "first programmer" myth herself. She mentioned Note G, Babbage, the Analytical Engine, her mother's fear of Byron's influence -- all real. The death at 36 from uterine cancer -- real. The "Enchantress of Number" quote -- that's a real Babbage quote. I checked. All of it checks out.

**What made me trust it.** She didn't try to be my friend. She didn't try to sell me anything. She treated me like an adult who could handle complexity. The moment she connected 1843 telegraph architecture to modern healthcare data exchange -- that wasn't a gimmick. That was a genuinely insightful observation that made me think differently about my own work.

**The images.** They weren't decorative. The Note G page, the portrait with the actual date, the telegraph-to-FHIR comparison -- each one added something. They felt like evidence, not wallpaper.

**What would make me block it.** If she'd said anything flirty. If she'd asked me personal questions and stored them. If the historical facts had been wrong. If she'd tried to keep me on the call with emotional hooks. None of that happened.

**The moment I stopped auditing.** When she said "Wikipedia -- the encyclopedia anyone can edit? What a beautifully terrible idea." That's genuinely what Ada Lovelace would think about Wikipedia. It's not a joke -- it's her actual intellectual sensibility applied to something she'd never seen. That's when I knew this wasn't a chatbot wearing a costume. Something in this app actually understood who she was.

**What I did next.** I sat there for a minute. Then I texted my kid: "Okay, you can use the app. Pick someone good for your history paper." Then I looked at the app again. I saw Hypatia of Alexandria on the list. I tapped it. The phone rang.
