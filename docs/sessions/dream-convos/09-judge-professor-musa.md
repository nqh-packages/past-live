## Judge: Prof. James Okafor (History Professor) -- Mansa Musa

52, PhD African Diaspora history, 25 years teaching at Howard University. Most edtech AI makes him cringe -- inaccurate, oversimplified, Western-centric. Values primary source thinking, causal understanding, and connecting past to present. Pet peeve: AI that confidently states wrong facts.

---

*[Call connects. Ambient sound of a courtyard -- distant conversation, the rustle of fabric.]*

[IMAGE: Mansa Musa seated in a courtyard, richly dressed but not ostentatiously -- dignified. Warm light. A scholar's space, not a throne room.]

MUSA: "Peace be upon you. I am told you wish to speak with me. I am Musa, son of Abu-Bakr. You may know me as the ruler of Mali. What is it you wish to understand?"

STUDENT: "Hi! Yeah, I heard you were like the richest person ever. Is that true?"

MUSA: *[Laughs softly.]* "You know, people always begin there. With the gold. Let me ask you something first -- when you hear 'the richest person,' what picture comes to your mind?"

STUDENT: "I don't know... like, piles of gold? A huge palace?"

MUSA: "Interesting. You imagine a man sitting on gold, yes? Like a dragon on a pile of coins. But that is not how wealth worked in Mali. Let me tell you how I actually encountered gold every day. In my empire, gold was not something I hoarded. It flowed. It moved through trade routes -- from the mines of Bambuk and Bure, north across the Sahara, to the markets of Cairo, Tunis, Fez. My wealth was not a pile. It was a river. Do you understand the difference?"

STUDENT: "So... it was more about controlling trade than just having gold?"

MUSA: "Now you are thinking. Yes. I controlled the routes, the markets, the exchange. Salt came south from Taghaza -- do you know salt? In the Sahara, salt was as precious as gold. A block of salt in the south could be worth its weight in gold. My empire sat between the gold and the salt, and we governed the exchange. That is where power lived. Not in a vault."

STUDENT: "That's kind of like... controlling the supply chain?"

MUSA: "You could say that. But there is something else you should understand. I did not invent this system. My predecessors built it -- Sundiata Keita, the founder of Mali, organized the empire so that trade could move safely across vast distances. When I became Mansa -- that means 'king of kings' -- I inherited a system that already worked. My task was to strengthen it, to extend it, and to use it wisely. A ruler who claims he built everything from nothing is either a liar or a fool."

---

[CHOICE CARD]

**Mansa Musa is preparing for his famous pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324. He asks you:**

MUSA: "I am preparing for the Hajj -- the pilgrimage to Mecca that every Muslim who is able must undertake. I will bring a caravan of thousands. Now, I face a decision. Some of my advisors say I should travel modestly -- a king who shows too much wealth invites jealousy and danger. Others say a grand display will announce Mali to the world, bring us respect and trading partners. If you were advising me, what would you say?"

- A) Travel modestly -- protect the empire from unwanted attention
- B) Go grand -- show the world Mali's power
- C) Something in between -- enough to impress, not enough to destabilize

---

STUDENT: "I'd say go big. Show the world what Mali is."

MUSA: "That is what I chose. And I will tell you what happened -- because this is where the story gets complicated, and I want you to understand complications, not just victories."

MUSA: "I crossed the Sahara with perhaps sixty thousand people. I brought gold -- a great deal of gold. When we reached Cairo, I gave so generously to the poor and to the officials that I... well, I broke their economy."

STUDENT: "Wait, what? You broke their economy?"

MUSA: *[Pause.]* "Yes. I flooded Cairo with so much gold that its value dropped. Prices for everything rose. It took over a decade for Cairo's gold market to recover. Some historians estimate it was the only time in history one man's spending caused inflation across an entire region."

STUDENT: "So was that a mistake?"

MUSA: "That is the honest question, is it not? At the time, I was giving charity -- sadaqa -- which is a duty in Islam. I was being generous, which is what a righteous ruler does. But generosity without understanding the system you are generous within... that can cause harm. I learned something in Cairo. On my way back, I actually borrowed gold from Egyptian merchants to try to correct what I had done. I wanted to buy gold back, raise its price again."

STUDENT: "So you tried to fix it?"

MUSA: "I tried. Whether I succeeded fully, that is debated. But here is what I want you to sit with: a single action -- one caravan, one act of generosity -- reshaped the economy of a region I had no intention of harming. History is full of consequences that the people who set them in motion never intended. That is one of the hardest things to understand about the past. People are not villains or heroes. They are people making decisions with incomplete knowledge, and the world responds in ways they cannot predict."

---

STUDENT: "So what happened when you got back to Mali?"

MUSA: "Ah, now you are asking the question most people forget to ask. Everyone talks about my pilgrimage. Few ask what I did with what I learned. I brought back scholars, architects, and books. I had met the poet and architect Abu Ishaq al-Sahili in Mecca, and I invited him to return with me. He designed the great mosque at Timbuktu -- the Djinguereber Mosque -- and helped transform the city into a center of learning."

STUDENT: "Timbuktu was a real place? I always thought it was just like... a word for 'far away.'"

MUSA: *[A long pause.]* "That is something I hear often, and it troubles me. Timbuktu was one of the great intellectual cities of the world. By the time of my grandson's reign, the University of Sankore had scholars studying theology, law, mathematics, astronomy, medicine. Manuscripts -- tens of thousands of manuscripts -- were written and collected there. Many survive to this day. When Europeans later said 'Timbuktu' to mean 'nowhere,' they were erasing a city that was educating scholars while many European cities were still struggling with basic literacy. That erasure is not accidental. It serves a story -- that knowledge and civilization flowed in only one direction. From Europe outward. That story is wrong."

STUDENT: "I had no idea. Why don't we learn about that?"

MUSA: "That is a question for your teachers, not for me. But I will say this -- history is not what happened. History is what gets remembered, and who decides what is worth remembering. The manuscripts of Timbuktu exist. The ruins of Sankore exist. The question is whether the people writing your textbooks thought they mattered."

---

[CHOICE CARD]

MUSA: "Let me pose something to you. After my pilgrimage, the entire Mediterranean world knew about Mali. European mapmakers began putting my image on their maps -- the Catalan Atlas of 1375 shows me holding a gold nugget. Now, some historians say this attention was beneficial -- it brought trade and recognition. Others argue it invited eventual European interest in West African gold, which centuries later contributed to the exploitation of the continent. Which interpretation do you find more convincing?"

- A) The attention was positive -- Mali gained diplomatic and trade relationships
- B) The attention was dangerous -- it planted the seeds of future exploitation
- C) Both are true at the same time -- the same event had different consequences at different time scales

---

STUDENT: "I think... both? Like, it was good for Mali right then, but in the long run it told Europeans where the gold was."

MUSA: "That is a mature answer. History rarely gives us the satisfaction of a single clean reading. In my lifetime, the attention was advantageous. Mali was too powerful, too organized, too far from Europe for anyone to threaten us. But two hundred years later? Three hundred? The world shifted. The routes changed. The Portuguese came by sea. And yes -- they came looking for the gold they had read about on those maps. I could not have known that. No ruler can see three centuries ahead. But the chain of cause and effect is there, and understanding that chain -- that is what it means to think historically."

---

STUDENT: "This is nothing like what I expected. I thought you'd just tell me how rich you were."

MUSA: "If that is all you had learned, you would have learned nothing. Gold is not interesting. What people do with gold, why they do it, and what happens because of it -- that is where the real story lives. Remember: I was not a wealthy man who happened to be African. I was the ruler of a system -- a political, economic, intellectual system -- that was among the most advanced of its era. If you only remember the gold, you have missed everything that made the gold possible."

STUDENT: "I want to know more about Timbuktu and the manuscripts."

MUSA: "Then go looking. The manuscripts are still being studied -- the Ahmed Baba Institute, the Mamma Haidara Library. Scholars have been working to preserve them for decades. Some were smuggled out of Timbuktu in 2012 when the city was under threat. The knowledge survives because people risked their lives to protect it. That should tell you something about its value."

*[Pause.]*

MUSA: "I must go. But let me leave you with this: the next time someone tells you about 'the richest man in history,' ask them -- rich how? Rich in what? And what did that wealth make possible? Those are the questions that matter."

*[Call ends.]*

## Professor's Assessment

**Would I assign this to my students?** Yes.

**Why this conversation works:**

**Factual accuracy.** The Cairo inflation is documented by al-Umari, who interviewed Egyptian officials after Musa's visit. The Djinguereber Mosque, al-Sahili, the Catalan Atlas of 1375, the Sankore manuscripts -- all verifiable. Nothing in this conversation would make me write "INACCURATE" in the margin.

**Causal reasoning, not trivia.** The student walked in thinking "richest man ever" -- the Wikipedia headline -- and walked out understanding trade systems, unintended economic consequences, intellectual infrastructure, and the politics of historical memory. That is a journey from trivia to thinking.

**Choice moments without correct answers.** Both choice cards force the student to weigh perspectives, which is exactly what historians do. There is no multiple-choice trick. The character validates the student's answer while complicating it -- "That is a mature answer. History rarely gives us the satisfaction of a single clean reading." That is what good teaching sounds like.

**The character pushes back on assumptions without lecturing.** When the student says "Timbuktu was a real place?" Musa does not mock them. He pauses. He explains. And then he names the structural reason the student did not know -- that erasure serves a narrative. He teaches the student to question why they know what they know. That is historical thinking at the highest level.

**Honest about limitations.** Musa admits the Cairo inflation was a mistake. He says "I could not have known that. No ruler can see three centuries ahead." A character who is honest about his own blind spots teaches students that historical actors are not omniscient. That combats the Great Man Theory framing that most edtech reinforces.

**Non-Western without being tokenizing.** This is not "diversity content." This is a rigorous conversation about economics, religion, diplomacy, and epistemology that happens to center a West African empire -- because that is where the history actually happened.

**The standard this sets:** If the app consistently produces conversations at this level -- accurate history, sound pedagogy, causal reasoning, genuinely non-Western perspectives -- I would use it as a first encounter. The thing that makes a student curious enough to go read al-Umari. Good educational technology does not replace the teacher. It makes the student ready to be taught.
